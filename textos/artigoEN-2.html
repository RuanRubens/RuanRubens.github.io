Elite Squad: The Enemy Remains the Same

May 5, 2026

Elite Squad follows Captain Nascimento, a BOPE officer, in the late 1990s. The narrative shows drug trafficking brutality, police corruption, and the physical and psychological exhaustion of the unit.

BOPE is an elite unit of the State of Rio de Janeiro, created for combat operations in hard-to-access areas. BOPE’s actions are marked by the application of military strategies in policing, which characterizes it as a militarized police force.

Unlike Brazil, most police forces in the world are not militarized, that is, they do not have organization, training, and hierarchy based on the military model.

In 1966, an analysis was carried out in the United States, with an explicit reading of police activity in ghettos, using an analogy with Algeria under French occupation. The central idea was that:

Black ghettos in the United States were occupied territories. The police were not seen as a force of “law enforcement,” but as an occupying army that controlled, repressed, and monitored a colonized population.

This interpretation fit into the concept of internal colonialism, influenced by authors such as Frantz Fanon (especially "The Wretched of the Earth") and by Third World theorists, who saw minorities in central countries as colonized peoples within their own nations.

Repression increases throughout the country, in militarized subprefectures, however the drop in crime does not follow. What actually occurs is the increase in fear among the local population and hatred toward the police force itself.

The Fogo Cruzado Institute (a Brazilian NGO that monitors and publishes data on armed violence in the country) warns about police violence, especially in states with militarized police, such as Bahia. In 2023 alone, nearly 2,000 people were killed in police actions.

As shown in the image, 4 of the top 5 most violent cities in the country are in Bahia, which also leads the ranking of police lethality, with a 300% increase over 8 years.

Just like the same policies practiced in the film, which seeks to portray the 1990s, do not work or are not successful in reducing crime, the use of body cameras reduces, but does not manage to end police violence.

The enemy remains the same!
