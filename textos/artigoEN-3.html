Black Man and The Man Who is Black

May 7, 2026

In Django Unchained, there is a symbolic inversion of the subject and its attributes; in this sense, the film dramatizes what thinkers such as Fanon and Stuart Hall discuss: how Black identity is socially marked before any other characteristic.

The struggle for rights is also a struggle for the right to invert the inversion, to place the human first, without erasing racial difference, but without allowing it to become reductive or dehumanizing.

Linguistically, in Portuguese we would say “black man,” but in the universe of the film, which resembles our own, the dominant gaze reverses the order: what is seen first is the “black,” and only afterward the “man.” This inversion of priority reveals the mechanism of dehumanization.

The theory of Frantz Fanon, in Black Skin, White Masks, helps to understand this operation: the colonized, and especially the Black person, is not recognized as a universal subject, but reduced to their skin color, to the difference that separates them from the white norm.

In the film, Django is only treated as a man when Dr. King Schultz frees him and, above all, when he becomes functional to the logic of the bounty hunter. Before that, he is merely a “black slave,” an inferiorized category.

The adjective “black” takes the place of the noun, and “man” almost disappears. It is the cinematic illustration of inversion: he is not “a man who is black,” but “a black who, incidentally, is a man.”
